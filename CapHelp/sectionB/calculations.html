Calculations Used in CAPREP

The calculations performed in the Report program use the formulas below.

Capillary Flow Porometry

Pressure (PSI): P = Raw pressure data
Diameter (µm): Dia = (0.415 * Surface tension) / P
Wet Flow (L/min): QW = Raw wet flow data
Dry Flow (L/min): QD = Raw dry flow data
Cumulative Filter Flow %: CFF = (QW / QD) * 100
Filter Flow %: FFP = CFF - Last FFP
Pore Size Distribution: PSD = (FFP - Last FFP) / (Last Dia - Dia)
Average Diameter (µm): AD = (Dia + Last Dia) / 2

Gas Permeability

Differential Pres. (PSI): DP = (Raw pressure data) - (Atmospheric pressure)
Flow (cc/sec): F1 = Raw flow data
Flow per Area and Pressure: F2 = F1 / (DP * Pi * Dia2 * 250) (in L / (PSI*SqCm*Sec)
Flow per Area and Pressure: F3 = F2 * 60 (in L / (PSI*SqCm*Min)
Darcy's Permeability Constant: DPC = (8*F1*Length*Mu)/(Pi*Dia2(Atm*Atm-1)
Atm = (Raw pressure data) / (Atmospheric pressure)(in PSI)
Pi = 3.14159265

Mean Flow Pore Size
A saturated sample will pass air when the applied pressure exceeds the capillary attraction of the fluid in the pores, allowing flow to pass through. Smaller pores have a higher capillary attraction than larger pores. Therefore, smaller pores open at higher pressures.

By comparing the gas flow rates of a wet and dry sample at the same pressures, the percentage of flow passing through the pores larger than or equal to the specified size may be calculated from the pressure-size relationship. By increasing pressure in small steps, it is possible to determine by the flow contribution of very small pore size increments.

The pressure at which wet flow is one-half the dry flow is used to calculate the mean flow pore size using equation [1] in the above section labeled, Maximum Pore Size.

WESA Calculation
Volumetric, dynamic flow, and gravimetric methods area three traditional technologies in solid surface area analysis via adsorption. The first two methods (volumetric and dynamic flow) have been widely used because of the availability of commercial automated operating systems. Though there are significant differences among these techniques regarding the methodology of isotherm measurement they all utilize the same models to process the isotherm for surface area analysis. The most common one is Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) multilayer physical adsorption model. The BET theory is almost universally employed for total surface area measurement on various solids and/or powder materials.

An alternate approach for surface area analysis is the flow permeametry method which is probably the simplest experiment for surface area determination. In addition, this method is capable of measuring the external or envelope area of a powder sample, which is otherwise not readily available by any adsorption methods.

The most critical aspect of using flow permeametry to measure the surface area of a powder is the control of the flow pattern. Comparison studies were made between specific surface areas measured by conventional volumetric auto sorptometer and fluid permeameter methods and flow permeametry. It was found that for materials with a surface area of only several square meters per gram, such as most pharmaceutical, metal and ceramic powders, flow permeametry offers a simple, fast and reliable technology for surface area measurement.

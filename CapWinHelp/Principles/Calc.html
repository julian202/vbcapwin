Mean Flow Pore Size

A saturated sample will pass air when the applied pressure exceeds the capillary attraction of the fluid in the pores, allowing flow to pass through. Smaller pores have a higher capillary attraction than larger pores. Therefore, smaller pores open at higher pressures.

By comparing the gas flow rates of a wet and dry sample at the same pressures, the percentage of flow passing through the pores larger than or equal to the specified size may be calculated from the pressure-size relationship. By increasing pressure in small steps, it is possible to determine by the flow contribution of very small pore size increments.

The pressure at which wet flow is one-half the dry flow is used to calculate the mean flow pore size using equation [1] of the Maximum Pore Size section.

Average Particle Size and Fiber Diameter form ESA Calculations

In the Envelope Surface Area test, the surface area per unit mass (Sm) and the density of the particles (ρp) is calculated. From these two values, the average particle size (Dp) of a powder sample can be obtained if it can be assumed that all particles of the sample are spherical and are the same size. If you are able to assume that the fibers of the sample being used (should be cylindrical) have the same diameter and length, you can find the average fiber diameter (Df),using the true density of the fiber (ρf). The following two equations are used to find Dp and Df respectively:

Average Particle Size: Dp= 6/(Sm*ρp)

Average Fiber Diameter: Df= 4/(Sm* ρf).

WESA Calculation

Volumetric, dynamic flow, and gravimetric methods area three traditional technologies in solid surface area analysis via adsorption. The first two methods (volumetric and dynamic flow) have been widely used because of the availability of commercial automated operating systems. Though there are significant differences among these techniques regarding the methodology of isotherm measurement they all utilize the same models to process the isotherm for surface area analysis. The most common one is Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) multi layer physical adsorption model. The BET theory is almost universally employed for total surface area measurement on various solids and/or powder materials.

An alternate approach for surface area analysis is the flow permeametry method which is probably the simplest experiment for surface area determination. In addition, this method is capable of measuring the external or envelope area of a powder sample, which is otherwise not readily available by any adsorption methods.

The most critical aspect of using flow permeametry to measure the surface area of a powder is the control of the flow pattern. Comparison studies were made between specific surface areas measured by conventional volumetric auto sorptometer and fluid permeameter methods and flow permeametry. It was found that for materials with a surface area of only several square meters per gram, such as most pharmaceutical, metal and ceramic powders, flow permeametry offers a simple, fast and reliable technology for surface area measurement.
